Problem: Liquidity is low, find drivers.
Reasoning Steps:
Check the direct components of liquidity:

Start by reviewing the company's most liquid assets (e.g., cash, accounts receivable (AR), inventory, and short-term investments).
These are the core elements that contribute directly to the company's ability to meet short-term obligations.
If any of these components are unusually low or deteriorating, it would be an immediate driver of low liquidity.
If no direct issues are found, check liquidity measures:

If the direct components of liquidity seem in line, move on to broader liquidity measures like working capital and the current ratio.
The current ratio (current assets / current liabilities) should be above 1 for healthy liquidity.
Similarly, evaluate working capital (current assets - current liabilities). A negative or low working capital would suggest that the company might struggle to meet its short-term obligations, even if direct liquid assets look fine.
Investigate possible liquidity distortions, such as the conversion of long-term liabilities to short-term obligations:

If the current ratio and working capital both show weakness, investigate whether there are long-term liabilities being converted into short-term liabilities.
For example, a line of credit or long-term debt maturing within the next 12 months could shift what would traditionally be a long-term liability into a short-term one, exacerbating liquidity issues.
Conclusion: The maturing long-term debt or line of credit being classified as short-term liabilities can artificially reduce working capital, causing liquidity to appear lower than it would be otherwise. This conversion can cause the company to face an unexpectedly large near-term cash outflow, further straining liquidity.
This approach breaks down the reasoning into clear, sequential steps:

First, examining direct components of liquidity.
Then, considering broader liquidity measures like working capital and the current ratio.
Finally, investigating specific factors that could distort liquidity, such as the reclassification of liabilities.
This ensures you cover multiple angles and arrive at a comprehensive understanding of what’s driving low liquidity.
